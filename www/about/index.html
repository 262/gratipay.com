Our Mission

Gittip’s mission is to establish giving as our primary social interaction.

To this end we’re building a platform to support gift economies.

Gittips

Gittips (short for “gift tips”) are the basis for the Gittip gift economy platform. Gittips are small, anonymous, weekly gifts of money with no strings attached.

Imagine walking down the street and a bunch of your local townsfolk are lined up sitting there alongside a slew of celebrities and a random assortment of other humans. Each one is doing their own thing and has a jar out. You have a few bucks in your pocket and you’re in a good mood. Who would you tip?

There’s something called a genius grant that the MacArthur Foundation gives to about 30 people each year. It’s ten million dollars over five years, no strings attached. “Just keep being a genius. Please spend another five years making the world better for the rest of us.” The winners don’t even know they’re in the running until they get the phone call that they won.

Gittip is genius grants for the rest of us. Call them pretty-smart grants, or crowd-sourced genius grants.

Gittip is (eventually) for the local coffee shop owner that’s really doing a lot for your town. You’re a fan. You buy coffee from her, but you want to do something more—a little something, not a big deal. If a hundred other people in your town feel the same way, it starts to add up. Now she can offer free coffee on Sunday mornings. Or whatever. She knows what she’s doing. No strings attached.

Gittip is (eventually) for the musician who gives their music away for free. You love their work. You go to their shows, you actually pay for the downloads, but you want to make sure their family is covered. You want to chip in—a little something—so they’re freed up to make great music.

Gittip is (today) for your favorite open source developer. You study their code, and you implicitly trust any software they write. You want to free them up to write even more of it.

Gittip is voluntary welfare for productive people.

Gittip is Kickstarter for people (not just projects).

Gittip is Flattr for people (not just content).

Gittip is a gift economy platform implemented atop the global exchange economy.

Gift Economy Platform

A gift economy is “a mode of exchange that contrasts to market or commodity exchange. In principle, voluntary and recurring gift exchange both creates and circulates wealth and consumption goods and builds a community. In contrast to a market economy, informal custom governs gift exchange, rather than negotiated pricing.”

Gittip can be thought of as a gift economy in which the individual and society as a whole are the two parties in the gift exchange. The individual freely gives the fruits of their labor, whether it be open source software, music, etc., to all members of society as a whole. Society as a whole reciprocates, using money borrowed from the existing global exchange economy to accord social status to beneficent individuals.

Money has traditionally been used to encode the value of goods, but Gittip subverts money to encode the value of people, which sounds really scary and wrong. However, Gittip is radically democratic. People in Gittip are not a “human resource” over which a hierarchy exerts control. People in Gittip are ... people. Free, autonomous individuals. Free to make society better on their own terms, unencumbered by duty to a hierarchy. Free, of course, not to participate in Gittip.

The mission of Gittip is to establish giving as our primary social interaction. This already happens in myriad ways that have nothing to do with Gittip. Open source software is probably our best example of an existing large-scale gift economy, but of course at the level of friends and family we all participate in localized gift economies. Gittip exists to bridge the gap between existing and yet-to-emerge larger-scale gift economies and the global exchange economy. The hope is that by doing so we can “reverse the polarity” of our economy, so that voluntary cooperative giving becomes the global norm rather than coerced competitive taking.

Details

Gittips are weekly. One-off contributions aren’t enough.

Gittips are anonymous. The people you gittip aren’t told who tipped them. Also, you can’t gittip more than $1.28 a week. This way, gittips come with no strings attached. Alice works on whatever she wants to work on. Bob gives to whomever he wants to.

Gittips are public. The amount you give is private, but the total amount you receive is public. Gifts received closely reflect benefit to society, so calling out high receivers encourages true value creation. Alice is concretely rewarded by a thousand Bobs for contributing to society.

Gittips are distributed every Friday. Gittips come out of your current balance (the money people have gifted you), and if that’s not enough, Gittip can charge your credit card to make up the difference. We add an additional 30¢ + 3.9% fee when pulling money in from your credit card, to cover credit card processing (as well as hosting and other expenses). Recipients get the full face value of your gift to them. If you give someone $1.28, they will have $1.28 in their Gittip account every Friday until you run out of money.

For example, let’s say that you have $5 in your account, and you are set up to give $0.64 to 20 people. Here’s what we will charge your credit card on Friday:

Participants can withdraw funds from their Gittip account at any time, though this is currently a manual process.
