What’s a gittip?

Gittips (short for “gift tips”) are small gifts of money with no strings attached.

Imagine walking down the street and a bunch of your local townsfolk are lined up sitting there alongside a slew of celebrities and a random assortment of other humans. Each one is doing their own thing and has a jar out. You have a few bucks in your pocket and you’re in a good mood. Who would you tip?

There’s something called a genius grant that the MacArthur Foundation gives to about 30 people each year. It’s ten million dollars over five years, no strings attached. “Just keep being a genius. Please spend another five years making the world better for the rest of us.” The winners don’t even know they’re in the running until they get the phone call that they won.

Gittip is genius grants for the rest of us. Call them pretty-smart grants, or crowd-sourced genius grants.

Gittip is (eventually) for the local coffee shop owner that’s really doing a lot for your town. You’re a fan. You buy coffee from her, but you want to do something more—a little something, not a big deal. If a hundred other people in your town feel the same way, it starts to add up. Now she can offer free coffee on Sunday mornings. Or whatever. She knows what she’s doing. No strings attached.

Gittip is (eventually) for the musician who gives their music away for free. You love their work. You go to their shows, you actually pay for the downloads, but you want to make sure their family is covered. You want to chip in—a little something—so they’re freed up to make great music.

Gittip is (today) for your favorite open source hacker. You implicitly trust any software they write. You study their code. You want to free them up to write even more of it.

Gittip is Kickstarter for people (not just projects).

Gittip is Flattr for people (not just content).

Details

Gittips are weekly. One-off contributions aren’t enough.

Gittips are anonymous. The people you gittip aren’t told who tipped them. Also, you can’t gittip more than $24 a week. This way, gittips come with no strings attached.

Gittips are public. The total amount you give and the total amount you receive is public. Participants on both sides of the equation are rewarded publicly for their participation.

Gittips are distributed every Friday. Gittips come out of your current balance (the money people have gifted you), and if that’s not enough, Gittip can charge your credit card to make up the difference. We add an additional 30¢ + 3.9% fee when pulling money in from your credit card, to cover credit card processing (as well as hosting and other expenses). Recipients get the full face value of your gift to them. If you give someone $3, they will have $3 in their Gittip account every Friday until you run out of money.

For example, let’s say that you have $5 in your account, and you are set up to give $0.25 to 30 people. Here’s what we will charge your credit card on Friday:

x 30

-$ 2.50

Participants can withdraw funds from their Gittip account at any time, though this is currently a manual process.
